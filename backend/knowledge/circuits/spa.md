# Spa-Francorchamps Circuit Guide

## Circuit Overview

**Official Name:** Circuit de Spa-Francorchamps  
**Location:** Stavelot, Ardennes, Belgium  
**Circuit Type:** Permanent road course through public roads (partially)  
**Length:** 7.004 km (4.352 miles) — the longest circuit on the F1 calendar  
**Race Laps:** 44  
**Race Distance:** 308.052 km (191.415 miles)  
**Lap Record:** 1:46.286 — Valtteri Bottas (Mercedes, 2018)  
**DRS Zones:** 2 (Kemmel Straight after Raidillon; between Bus Stop chicane and La Source)  
**Corners:** 20  
**Average Speed:** Approximately 230-240 km/h  

## The Greatest Circuit in the World

Spa-Francorchamps is frequently cited by drivers, engineers, and fans as the greatest Formula 1 circuit in the world. Its combination of extreme high-speed corners, massive elevation changes, unpredictable weather, and historic significance creates a challenge unlike any other on the calendar.

The Ardennes forest surrounds the circuit, and the region's geography produces the micro-climate phenomenon that makes Spa uniquely dangerous and strategically complex. Rain can be falling at Eau Rouge while the back straight bakes in sunshine — occasionally within the same lap.

The modern Spa-Francorchamps layout was established through successive modifications to the original 1921 circuit, which covered 14.8 km of public roads. The current configuration opened in 1983 and has been refined multiple times since, most recently for the 2022 Belgian Grand Prix.

## Track Layout and Famous Corners

**La Source (Turn 1)**
The first corner after the start/finish straight — a tight, downhill hairpin. Heavy braking from high speed. La Source is one of the most important overtaking spots at Spa, particularly on the opening lap when cars are bunched together. The downhill approach makes braking particularly tricky.

**Eau Rouge / Raidillon (Turns 3-4)**
The most famous corner sequence in all of motorsport. Cars plunge downhill from La Source into the valley, hitting the fast left of Eau Rouge at the bottom, then immediately hurling up the right-left Raidillon on a steep uphill gradient. In modern F1 cars, this is taken flat-out — a genuinely terrifying piece of driving requiring complete commitment at over 300 km/h under severe G-forces.

Historically, Eau Rouge was one of the most respected and feared corners in F1. In the era before aerodynamics provided sufficient downforce, drivers had to lift. As cars became more aerodynamically sophisticated, more and more committed to flat. Ralf Schumacher's massive crash at the start of the 2002 Belgian GP — car destroyed, driver unharmed by a miracle — was one of several reminders that Eau Rouge demands respect.

The word "Raidillon" refers specifically to the steep climb after the valley. Eau Rouge is technically just the left-hander at the bottom. The terms are often used interchangeably.

**Kemmel Straight**
The long straight after Raidillon climbing further uphill before leveling out. One of the most spectacular DRS zones in F1 — cars exit Raidillon already at 300+ km/h and continue accelerating up the hill, reaching 340+ km/h. Following cars with DRS activated appear to catapult past rivals in a way rarely seen elsewhere.

**Les Combes (Turns 5-6-7)**
The braking zone at the end of Kemmel Straight — one of the key overtaking spots. A right-left chicane demanding precise braking from enormous speeds. The approach from Eau Rouge through Kemmel makes this one of the most dramatic pass locations on the calendar.

**Malmedy, Rivage (Turns 8-10)**
A complex sequence through the back of the circuit. Rivage is a slow hairpin providing another passing opportunity and the beginning of the bus stop section.

**Pouhon (Turns 11-12)**
A fast, double-apex left-hand curve through the back section of the circuit. Taken at very high speed with significant downforce loading. One of the most demanding corners at Spa — drivers must commit early and hold through the entire sequence. Getting Pouhon wrong usually means a significant off.

**Blanchimont (Turn 17)**
A very fast right-hander in the circuit's back straight section, typically taken flat-out. The approach is committed at 290+ km/h. Any aerodynamic instability here is extremely consequential.

**Bus Stop Chicane (Turns 18-19)**
The final chicane before the long pit straight. A slow, sharp left-right that rewards drivers who get the exit right. The kerbs here have been contentious — drivers sometimes cut through the Bus Stop to maintain speed, leading to track limits investigations.

## Micro-Climate: Rain in One Sector, Dry in Another

Spa's position in the Ardennes creates genuinely different weather in different parts of the circuit. A front approaching from the west may bring rain to the Raidillon-Kemmel area while La Source and the pit straight remain bone dry. Drivers can exit the pit lane on dry tyres, enter Eau Rouge in the rain, and return to dry conditions by Blanchimont.

This creates extraordinary tyre compound dilemmas. Teams watch weather radar intensely throughout sessions. Making the wrong tyre call — fitting wets when rain stops at Turn 1 but continues at Turn 7 — can destroy a race. The 2021 Belgian GP was declared after two formation laps behind the safety car due to relentless rain — Verstappen was classified winner, Perez second, Hamilton third after qualifying order was used. Massive controversy as full points were awarded for what was not a real race.

The 2023 Belgian GP weekend saw classic Spa variability, with qualifying and race day conditions vastly different, producing strategy chaos and significant driver errors.

## Elevation Changes

The 100+ meter elevation change around the circuit creates aerodynamic and braking challenges that don't exist at flat circuits. Cars are heavier going downhill under braking (apparent weight increases), lighter uphill under acceleration. The Raidillon climb in particular means cars are generating aerodynamic loads on an upward gradient — the combination requires confidence in the car's mechanical and aerodynamic grip simultaneously.

## Setup Characteristics

Spa requires a balanced, medium-to-high downforce setup. Low downforce is inadvisable given the commitment required through Eau Rouge and Pouhon. High downforce hurts on Kemmel Straight. The compromise favors efficient wings that generate clean downforce without excessive drag.

Power is important at Spa — the combination of Kemmel Straight and the other longer sections rewards high-power power units. The Mercedes power unit domination of 2014-2021 showed particularly strongly at Spa.

## Tyre Strategy

With a long lap (7.004 km), fewer laps are required (44), but the circuit's variety of corner types creates moderate degradation. Rear tyre wear is notable through the high-speed Raidillon sequence and through Pouhon. Front wear is significant in the slow chicanes.

One-stop strategies are viable in most conditions. Two-stop is forced by high degradation or by safety car events creating free stop opportunities. Weather-induced compound changes can transform a planned one-stop into a three or four-stop race as teams switch between wet, intermediate, and dry compounds.

## Notable Moments

**1998 Belgian GP:** Michael Schumacher in arguably his greatest single-lap performance. Starting 14th after a collision, he was back in 2nd within 25 laps in wet-to-dry conditions.

**2008 Belgian GP:** Hamilton passed Räikkönen around the outside of La Source on the final lap — then gave the position back after gaining advantage by cutting the chicane — then was penalized for it anyway. One of the most controversial stewards' decisions in the modern era.

**2021 Belgian GP:** The "race" that wasn't — two laps behind the safety car, full points awarded, massive public anger at F1 for proceeding at all in the torrential conditions.
