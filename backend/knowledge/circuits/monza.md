# Monza Circuit Guide

## Circuit Overview

**Official Name:** Autodromo Nazionale di Monza  
**Location:** Monza, Lombardy, Italy  
**Circuit Type:** Permanent road course (with banked oval section unused since 1969)  
**Length:** 5.793 km (3.600 miles)  
**Race Laps:** 53  
**Race Distance:** 306.720 km (190.567 miles)  
**Lap Record:** 1:21.046 — Rubens Barrichello (Ferrari, 2004)  
**DRS Zones:** 2 (main straight; between Lesmo 2 and Variante Ascari)  
**Corners:** 11  
**Average Speed:** Approximately 255-265 km/h — the fastest average of any F1 race  

## The Temple of Speed

Monza is Formula 1's fastest circuit, earning its "Temple of Speed" nickname through decades of producing the highest average lap speeds on the calendar. The circuit's combination of three long straights connected by slow chicanes means cars spend the majority of the lap at full throttle, with top speeds regularly exceeding 350 km/h.

The Autodromo Nazionale di Monza opened in 1922 and hosted the first Italian Grand Prix. It has been on the Formula 1 calendar since the championship's inaugural 1950 season and has missed only two Grands Prix (1980 at Imola, 2020 in the Monza park bubble race held at Mugello... actually 2020 was held at Monza as scheduled). Its longevity makes it the spiritual home of Ferrari and Italian motorsport.

## Track Layout and Famous Corners

**Variante del Rettifilo (Turn 1-2)**
The first chicane after the start/finish straight, arriving from the pit straight at over 340 km/h. The heaviest braking zone of the lap. High lock-up risk under the massive deceleration required. Frequent overtaking opportunity as the long straight provides DRS and slipstream opportunities.

**Curva Grande (Turn 3)**
A fast, sweeping right-hander taken flat-out or close to it in modern F1 cars. Tests aerodynamic efficiency at high speed. The low-downforce Monza setup means cars are on the limit here.

**Variante della Roggia (Turn 4-5)**
A second chicane in the first sector. Cars bounce over the sausage kerbs that limit cutting. Another heavy braking zone, but shorter approach than Turn 1.

**Lesmo 1 and Lesmo 2 (Turns 7-8)**
Two flowing medium-to-fast right-hand corners through the park section of the circuit. Lesmo 1 opens up into Lesmo 2; getting the Lesmo 2 exit right is critical for speed onto the back straight. The banking and camber changes make these corners particularly demanding.

**Variante Ascari (Turns 10-11-12)**
A high-speed chicane named after Alberto Ascari, the two-time world champion who died here testing a Ferrari in 1955. Entered at approximately 290 km/h, requiring one of the most precise braking points of the lap. The chicane configuration has been modified several times over the decades.

**Curva Parabolica / Curva Alboreto (Turn 11)**
The final corner — a long, sweeping right-hander leading onto the main start/finish straight. Renamed Curva Alboreto in memory of Michele Alboreto in 2020. Exit speed from the Parabolica is critical: a car exiting 5 km/h faster will be nearly 25m ahead at the end of the main straight, crucial for slipstream and DRS positioning.

## Low-Downforce Setup: The Monza Paradox

Running minimum downforce at Monza creates an unusual dynamic. Cars that are aerodynamically very efficient — that can generate good downforce with less drag — perform relatively better here than at high-downforce tracks. Historically this has meant Mercedes power units excelled (lower drag from more efficient packaging), while naturally high-drag designs struggled.

Teams fit specially made low-downforce rear wings at Monza (often called "Monza wings" or "low-drag kits"), with shallow angles and reduced wing area. The front wing is also trimmed. Some teams fit a special low-drag body kit to the floor and sidepods. The entire car philosophy shifts from "generate downforce" to "minimize resistance."

The trade-off: without downforce, the car is nervous through the Lesmos, unstable in the Parabolica, and requires exceptional commitment from the driver. Historic Monza crashes have often been attributed to the knife-edge nature of low-downforce setups.

## Slipstream Battles and Drag Racing on the Straights

Monza produces some of Formula 1's most exciting qualifying and racing due to slipstream dynamics. In qualifying, drivers jockey for position on the in-lap, deliberately slowing down to get into another car's slipstream for a speed boost on the flying lap. The result is chaotic slow laps with drivers playing cat-and-mouse, sometimes dramatically — Verstappen and Hamilton famously had a confrontation in 2020 qualifying where neither wanted to lead on the final flying lap.

In the race, slipstream chains form on the long straights. Multiple cars drafting each other can be significantly faster than a lone car at the front, creating dynamic battles where the lead changes through the DRS activation zones.

## Tyre Strategy

Monza's tyre strategy is typically straightforward due to the circuit's characteristics:

- Low cornering loads (slow chicanes with short duration, fast corners at low downforce) produce relatively low tyre degradation
- Hard compounds are preferred to ensure one-stop viability
- The long straights mean thermal degradation from friction is modest compared to high-cornering-load circuits

Most races feature a one-stop strategy, though the chicane kerbs can cause flat spots, forcing unplanned stops. Strategy in a Monza race is often determined by the safety car — Monza has one of the higher safety car probability rates due to the high speeds and chicane-induced incidents.

## The Tifosi

No description of Monza is complete without its fans. The Tifosi — Ferrari's passionate Italian supporters — pack the grandstands in red. When Ferrari wins at Monza (a relatively rare event in recent decades), the scenes of fans flooding the track, climbing the podium barriers, and celebrating are among Formula 1's most extraordinary spectacles.

The emotional weight of racing at Monza for Ferrari is unique in the sport. Ferrari's relationship with the Autodromo stretches back to Enzo Ferrari himself — losing at Monza carries psychological weight beyond the championship mathematics.

## Notable Moments

**1971 Italian GP:** Peter Gethin's winning margin of 0.01 seconds — the closest finish in F1 history. Five cars separated by just 0.61 seconds after 55 laps.

**2019 Italian GP:** Charles Leclerc won for Ferrari at their home circuit, Ferrari's first Monza win since 2010, prompting scenes of mass celebration.

**2020 Italian GP:** Pierre Gasly won with AlphaTauri, one of the most shocking results in recent F1 history, benefiting from Lewis Hamilton's pit lane exit penalty and the unusual circumstances of a race with multiple safety car interventions.

**2023 Italian GP:** Carlos Sainz won for Ferrari at Monza, again producing emotional scenes among the Tifosi.
