# F1 Race Weekend Format: How a Grand Prix Weekend Works

## Overview

A Formula 1 Grand Prix weekend typically spans Thursday through Sunday (or Friday through Sunday for European events). The schedule includes official sessions, media obligations, press conferences, and fan activities, but the on-track program follows a defined structure set by the FIA and Formula 1 Management.

## Standard (Non-Sprint) Weekend Format

### Thursday / Friday: Media Day and FP1
Thursday at most European races and Friday at other venues is the official media day — mandatory press conferences for selected drivers and team principals. No on-track activity.

**Free Practice 1 (FP1)**
- Duration: 60 minutes
- Timing: Friday (or Thursday at Monaco)
- Purpose: First on-track running of the weekend. Teams check car setup, run aero correlation programs, evaluate tyre compounds, and collect baseline data.
- Tyre rules: Teams may use any combination of dry compounds supplied plus a set of wet/intermediate if conditions require. No restrictions on number of sets used within allocation.
- FP1 mandate: Since 2022, teams must run a "rookie driver" (fewer than 3 starts) in at least 2 FP1 sessions per season per car.

**Free Practice 2 (FP2)**
- Duration: 60 minutes
- Timing: Friday afternoon
- Purpose: Race simulation runs and longer tyre comparison programs. Teams often run one driver on a race simulation (many consecutive laps at representative pace) while the other evaluates qualifying setups.

### Saturday: FP3 and Qualifying

**Free Practice 3 (FP3)**
- Duration: 60 minutes
- Timing: Saturday morning
- Purpose: Final setup adjustments before qualifying. Teams refine the car's balance based on FP1 and FP2 data. Usually involves some qualifying simulation runs.

**Qualifying**
The knockout qualifying format was introduced in 2006 and has been the standard format since, replacing the old single-lap qualifying and aggregate-lap formats of the early 2000s.

Three segments eliminate drivers progressively:

**Q1 (18 minutes)**
All 20 drivers compete. The five slowest drivers are eliminated and start the race from positions 16th through 20th (in the order of their best Q1 times). The 15 fastest progress to Q2.

Q1 tyre rule: Drivers may use any available dry compound. The times set in Q1 are not often relevant to final grid positions for the front runners, who use them primarily as a warm-up.

**Q2 (15 minutes)**
The 15 remaining drivers compete. The five slowest are eliminated and start from 11th through 15th. The 10 fastest progress to Q3.

Q2 tyre rule: Since 2014, drivers who advance from Q2 to Q3 must start the race on the same compound they used to set their best Q2 lap time. This creates strategic differentiation — teams must choose between using the fastest (soft) compound in Q2, locking them into that compound for the race start, or using a harder compound (sacrificing Q2 pace) to have strategic flexibility at the race start.

**Q3 (12 minutes)**
The final 10 drivers compete for pole position. All drivers are given two sets of soft tyres. There are no restrictions on what they use these sets for.

The standard approach is a warm-up lap (lap 1), a timed flying lap (lap 2), a cool-down in-lap (lap 3), and then a final flying lap attempt (laps 4-5) before the session ends. The driver posting the fastest single lap wins pole position.

### Sunday: The Race

**Formation Lap**
All cars complete one full circuit of the track behind a safety car (in some circumstances) or under their own power, warming tyres and brakes. Drivers may weave, accelerate hard, and brake firmly to bring systems to temperature.

**Race Start**
Cars line up on the grid in qualifying order. Five red lights illuminate one by one, then all five extinguish simultaneously — this is the signal to go. The time between the final light and the lights going out varies randomly (typically 0.2 to 3 seconds) to prevent drivers from anticipating the start.

**Race Distance**
The minimum race distance is 305km (except Monaco at 260km). The number of laps varies by circuit length. If the race has not been completed after 2 hours (or 3 hours in rare circumstances with stoppages), it is ended at the end of the current lap.

**Minimum Tyre Requirement**
Drivers must use at least two different dry tyre compounds unless the race begins or spends significant time under wet conditions, in which case the requirement may be waived.

## Sprint Weekend Format (Selected Circuits)

Since 2021, a subset of events (6 per season from 2023) use the Sprint format, which replaces FP2 with Sprint Qualifying and adds a Sprint race on Saturday.

### Friday
**FP1** — Standard 60-minute free practice session. The only free practice of the weekend.

**Sprint Qualifying (SQ)**
Introduced in 2023 to replace the original "Sprint Shootout." Three knockout segments similar to standard qualifying but shorter:
- SQ1: 12 minutes, eliminates 5 drivers
- SQ2: 10 minutes, eliminates 5 more
- SQ3: 8 minutes, sets the Sprint race grid (top 10)

All drivers use soft tyres in SQ3. No Q2 compound rule — sprint tyre choices do not affect Sunday race start.

### Saturday
**Sprint Race**
- Distance: Approximately 100km (roughly one-third of the Grand Prix distance)
- Duration: Around 30 minutes of racing
- Points: Awarded to top 8 finishers (8-7-6-5-4-3-2-1)
- No mandatory pit stop requirement
- No tyre compound rule (teams typically run one set of softs)
- Safety car and VSC rules apply

**Qualifying (Standard)**
Full Q1/Q2/Q3 knockout qualifying for the Grand Prix. Results set the Sunday race grid. Q2 compound rule applies.

### Sunday
**Grand Prix** — Standard race format.

## Parc Fermé Rules

Parc fermé (literally "closed park") is a regulatory state affecting the car between qualifying and the race. Once a car sets a timed lap in qualifying, it enters parc fermé conditions:

- Teams may not make setup changes beyond a defined list of permitted adjustments
- Permitted adjustments include: nose height change (within legal limits), front and rear wing angle within a declared range, brake pads, brake ducts, tyre selection, fuel load
- Prohibited changes include: suspension geometry changes, floor alterations, significant aerodynamic modifications

The parc fermé intent is to ensure the car in the race is essentially the same car that set the qualifying time. This prevents teams from running a highly specialized qualifying configuration and then reverting to a different race configuration overnight.

A driver who makes an unauthorized parc fermé change must start from the pit lane rather than their grid position.

On sprint weekends, parc fermé applies differently — after Sprint Qualifying, teams cannot make changes between Sprint Qualifying and the Sprint race, but are permitted a setup window between the Sprint and Sunday's Grand Prix Qualifying.

## Key Tyre Allocation Rules

Each driver receives a fixed allocation of tyre sets per weekend:
- **Standard weekend:** 13 sets (2 hard, 3 medium, 8 soft) allocated for the whole weekend; 1 hard and 1 medium must be returned unused before qualifying; 2 sets must be reserved for Q3 if the driver reaches Q3
- **Sprint weekend:** Modified allocation due to the abbreviated practice and the Sprint race consuming additional sets

Pirelli monitors tyre usage carefully and publishes which compounds are available at each event weeks in advance, allowing teams to plan their compound strategy before arriving at the circuit.
