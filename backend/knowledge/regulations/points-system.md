# The F1 Points System: How Championships Are Won

## Current Race Points (2010–Present)

The current points system awards points to the top ten finishers in each Grand Prix:

| Position | Points |
|----------|--------|
| 1st      | 25     |
| 2nd      | 18     |
| 3rd      | 15     |
| 4th      | 12     |
| 5th      | 10     |
| 6th      | 8      |
| 7th      | 6      |
| 8th      | 4      |
| 9th      | 2      |
| 10th     | 1      |

Drivers finishing 11th or lower score no championship points.

## The Fastest Lap Bonus Point

One additional point is awarded to the driver who sets the fastest lap of the race, subject to two conditions:

1. The driver must finish the race in the top 10 (i.e., they must score at least 1 point from finishing position)
2. The fastest lap must be set on the final lap or any lap during the race (not limited to the final lap)

This rule was reintroduced in 2019 after a long absence. It encourages teams to pit for fresh tyres late in a race to chase the fastest lap, adding a tactical layer — particularly if a driver is safely in a position where the loss of track position from a pit stop does not threaten their finish position.

The fastest lap point is sometimes decisive in championship fights. In 2019, Lewis Hamilton's additional fastest lap points contributed to his championship margin. Teams explicitly model fastest lap probability into their late-race strategy.

## Sprint Race Points (2021–Present)

Sprint races are shorter races (approximately 100km, around 30 minutes) held on Saturday at selected circuits per season. They award points to the top eight finishers:

| Position | Sprint Points |
|----------|---------------|
| 1st      | 8             |
| 2nd      | 7             |
| 3rd      | 6             |
| 4th      | 5             |
| 5th      | 4             |
| 6th      | 3             |
| 7th      | 2             |
| 8th      | 1             |

Sprint points are added to the drivers' and constructors' championship totals. In a season with 6 sprint events, a maximum of 48 additional sprint points are available. This is small relative to the 25 points per Grand Prix but can be meaningful in close title fights.

The sprint race format has been revised since introduction. Initially (2021-2022), sprint results set the grid for Sunday's race. From 2023, qualifying sessions determine the Sunday grid regardless of sprint results, and the sprint is treated as a standalone points event.

## The Constructors' Championship

The Constructors' Championship (or World Constructors' Championship) is calculated by adding the race points scored by both of a team's drivers in every race throughout the season.

For example, if Driver A finishes 2nd (18 points) and Driver B finishes 5th (10 points), the constructor scores 28 points from that race.

There is no separate constructors' points table — every point a driver scores is also a constructor point. Teams that frequently have one driver outperform the other lose constructors' points compared to teams with both drivers consistently in the points.

The Constructors' Championship carries significant financial implications: the prize money distributed by Formula 1 Management (FOM) at the end of the season is directly linked to constructors' championship position, with higher positions receiving substantially more. This makes the Constructors' Championship critical for team finances, affecting budgets for development and driver contracts.

## Maximum Points Available Per Season

In a full season with 24 Grand Prix events and 6 Sprint events:
- Race points maximum: 24 × 26 (25 + fastest lap) = 624 points
- Sprint points maximum: 6 × 8 = 48 points
- **Total maximum: 672 points**

In practice, no driver has approached the theoretical maximum. The realistic maximum accounting for consistent top-2 finishes is significantly lower.

## Historical Points Systems

F1 has changed its points allocation multiple times:

**1950-1959:** Points to top 5 (8-6-4-3-2) plus 1 for fastest lap. Drivers dropped their worst results under a counting system that limited the number of results that counted toward the title.

**1960:** Fastest lap point removed.

**1961-1990:** Points to top 6 (9-6-4-3-2-1). This system famously applied during the Senna-Prost era. The gap between 1st (9 pts) and 2nd (6 pts) was proportionally smaller than the modern 25-18 gap, meaning championships were decided more on consistency than dominant wins.

**1991-2002:** Same as 1961-1990 (9-6-4-3-2-1). Under this system, winning the championship required very consistent top-6 finishes across the season.

**2003-2009:** Points to top 8 (10-8-6-5-4-3-2-1). The winner-to-second gap narrowed further. Kimi Räikkönen won the 2007 championship over Hamilton and Alonso by a single point — both rivals' DNFs proved decisive.

**2010–Present:** Current 25-18-15-12-10-8-6-4-2-1 system, designed to reward race wins more heavily than the previous system. The 2009 system was seen as insufficiently rewarding wins; the 2010 change significantly widened the gap between 1st and 2nd.

**2019–Present addition:** Fastest lap bonus point reintroduced.

## Notable Championship Points Mathematics

**2007 (Räikkönen champion):** Räikkönen 110 pts, Hamilton 109, Alonso 109. The smallest margin in championship history — one point.

**2008 (Hamilton champion):** Hamilton's Glock overtake on the final corner of the final lap of the final race in Brazil gave him the 5th place (4 points) he needed over Massa (who won the race). Championship margin: 1 point.

**2021 (Verstappen champion):** Verstappen and Hamilton finished on 369.5 points each — the only tie in championship history. The title was decided by the tiebreaker rule of most race wins: Verstappen 10, Hamilton 8.

**2023 (Verstappen dominant):** Verstappen scored 575 points from 22 races, winning 19 of them — the most dominant single-season points total in the modern era.

## Points and Dropped Results (Historical)

In the 1950s and 1960s, drivers were required to drop their worst results from the season, with only a set number of best results counting toward the title. This was designed to reduce the impact of mechanical failures beyond the driver's control. The dropped results system was abolished in 1991, meaning every race result counts from that year forward, increasing the importance of reliability and consistent scoring over lucky wins.
